Use this playbook when your team wants to convert event activity into better conversations.

## What is different about event signals

Event signals are time-bound. Their strength comes from shared context and urgency.

The biggest mistake is waiting until the event memory fades, then sending generic follow-up that could have gone to anyone.

## Signals that matter most

Prioritize in this order:

1. Pre-event engagement with agenda, speaker, or topic posts
2. Live engagement during a session or around an event theme
3. Post-event takeaways, recaps, or topic discussion
4. Multiple people from a target account engaging around the same event
5. Speaker or panel engagement tied closely to your offer

## Work the event in three phases

### Pre-event

Use this window to book meetings or start conversations before the event starts.

- watch who engages with speaker announcements, agenda posts, and event topics
- reach out to strong-fit leads with a lightweight note
- aim to set up a short meeting or a hallway conversation, not a full pitch

### During the event

This is the fastest-moving phase.

- monitor live posts, comments, and takeaways
- use LinkedIn for real-time, conversational follow-up
- keep the message short and tied to the exact session or topic

### Post-event

This is where most teams slow down too much.

- send the first follow-up within 24 to 48 hours
- reference the exact topic, session, or takeaway
- add one useful angle or resource instead of sending a generic “great meeting you” note

## Best channel and timing

- LinkedIn during the event and right after it
- Email when you want to send something more substantive after the event

If more than a week has passed, the event context is no longer doing much work for you.

## Suggested sequence

1. Pre-event: one lightweight setup note
2. During event: short LinkedIn touch tied to the live topic
3. Within 48 hours after: email with one specific takeaway or resource
4. Days 4 to 7 after: one follow-up with a new angle

## Example post-event email

```text
Subject: [session topic] at [event]

Hey [Name],

Saw your takeaway on [session topic] at [event].

The point about [specific idea] is something we keep seeing with [their company type] teams as well.

If useful, I can send over a short breakdown of how teams are handling it.
```

## Common mistakes

- waiting too long after the event
- sending the same follow-up to everyone who attended
- referencing the event without any real substance
- building the post-event sequence only after the event is over
- ignoring strong pre-event signals that could have created meetings earlier

## What good looks like

- strong event signals are worked quickly
- the message references a real session, topic, or takeaway
- your best event follow-up feels like continuation, not re-introduction
