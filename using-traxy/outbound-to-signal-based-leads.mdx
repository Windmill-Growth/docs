Use this guide when `traxy` surfaces a qualified lead or qualified engagement and you want to turn that signal into a real conversation.

## Quick start

- Work the freshest signals first. Comments, shares, repeat engagement, and company clusters beat one-off reactions.
- Use the signal as context, not surveillance. Reference the topic unless the engagement was explicit and substantive.
- Start with an interest-based ask, not a meeting request.
- Use email for stronger, clearer signals and LinkedIn for lighter or more conversational first touches.
- Measure signal type, freshness, reply rate, and meeting rate every week so you know which signals are worth your team’s time.

## What makes signal-based outbound different

Signal-based outbound works because you already have a real reason to reach out. The lead engaged with a topic, post, or idea that matches your ICP.

That changes your job. You do not need to invent relevance. You need to:

- move while the signal is still fresh
- use the exact topic as your angle
- keep the message short and specific
- ask for a small next step

<Tip>
  Treat the signal as context, not as surveillance. Mention only the detail that makes your outreach relevant.
</Tip>

## Start with the best signals first

Prioritize leads when you see:

- recent qualified engagement on a post tied closely to your offer
- repeat engagement from the same person
- strong ICP fit plus recent engagement
- comments or shares that show clear interest in the topic
- multiple engaged people from the same company

Deprioritize leads when you see:

- weak ICP fit
- old signals with no recent activity
- engagement on broad content with no clear business angle
- one-off low-context reactions you cannot turn into a relevant message

## Signal freshness and decay

How fast you move matters almost as much as what you say.

| Tier | Window | What it means | How to work it |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hot | 0-48 hours | The signal is still current and likely top of mind. | Reach out the same day or next business morning. Use the topic directly. |
| Warm | 3-7 days | Still relevant, but no longer immediate. | Send a clear first touch with one strong angle. |
| Cooling | 8-14 days | The signal is fading. | Reach out only if ICP fit is strong or the engagement was substantive. Reference the topic more broadly. |
| Cold | 15+ days | The signal is no longer timely on its own. | Treat it like cold outbound with context. Do not lean hard on the original action. |

<Info>
  If you are working signals older than two weeks, prioritize speed over polish on your next fresh signals. The stale ones are already losing their advantage.
</Info>

## Conflicting signals

Not every lead is cleanly high or low priority. Use this rule set when fit and engagement do not line up.

| ICP fit | Signal strength | What to do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strong | Strong | Highest priority. Move fast. Use the clearest channel for the context. |
| Strong | Light | Still worth pursuing. Soften the opener and reference the topic rather than the exact action. LinkedIn-first often works well here. |
| Weak | Strong | Proceed carefully. The interest is real, but the buying fit may not be. Check whether the engagement reveals a real use case before you spend too much time. |
| Weak | Light | Skip it. This is what deprioritize means in practice. |

## Reference the signal without sounding invasive

Use the signal to explain why your message is relevant. Do not use it to prove that you tracked every action.

A good rule:

- if the lead commented, shared, or engaged repeatedly, you can be more direct
- if the lead only gave a light reaction, reference the topic instead of the exact action

Better:

- You were engaging with a conversation on [topic].
- Your comment on [topic] stood out to me.
- It looked like [topic] is relevant to your team right now.

Avoid:

- I saw you liked my post at 9:14 AM.
- I noticed every reaction you left on our content.
- I tracked your recent activity and wanted to reach out.

## Choose the right channel

### Use email when you want:

- more room for context
- a stronger business case
- an easy reply path
- a message that can stand on its own

### Use LinkedIn when you want:

- a lighter first touch
- to follow up on content or thought leadership
- to stay close to the topic they engaged with
- to warm up a future email conversation

### Use both when:

- the lead is a strong ICP match
- the signal is recent
- you can keep each touch short and different

Do not copy the same message into both channels.

## Use a simple workflow

1. Review the lead in `traxy`.
   Check the ICP fit, the topic that triggered interest, and whether the signal is still fresh.
2. Pick one angle.
   Choose one problem, one outcome, or one point from the content they engaged with.
3. Start with the lightest credible message.
   If the signal is strong, email can work first. If the signal is lighter, LinkedIn is often a better first touch.
4. Follow up with new value.
   Add a new example, insight, or question. Do not just ask whether they saw your last message.
5. Stop when the fit or interest is low.
   Signal-based outbound works best when you stay selective.

## Email best practices

Use email when you want a clear, focused business message.

### Keep the message short

Short emails usually work better than long explanations. Keep the message easy to scan and centered on one idea.

A strong signal-based email usually includes:

- a short subject line
- a first sentence tied to the signal
- one clear problem or outcome
- a low-friction call to action

### Use an interest-based call to action

Your first ask does not need to be a meeting.

Better first asks include:

- open to seeing how other teams handle this?
- worth sending over the short playbook we use here?
- is this something your team is actively working on?

These asks are easier to answer than a direct calendar request.

### Keep deliverability clean

If you send outbound at volume, use a properly authenticated sending domain and keep complaint rates low. Platform rules for bulk email have become stricter, especially for Gmail and Yahoo inboxes.

<Info>
  If you run larger outbound programs, review the official sender guidance from Google and Yahoo before you scale.
</Info>

### Example email framework

```text
Subject: [topic]

Hi [First name],

You engaged with a post on [topic], so I thought I would reach out directly.

Teams like [their team or company type] usually run into [specific problem] when this becomes a priority. We help with [specific outcome].

Would it be useful if I sent over the short version of how we approach this?
```

If the signal was only a light reaction, soften the opener. For example:

```text
You were following a conversation on [topic], so I thought I would reach out directly.
```

### Conversational version

Use this style when you want the message to feel more like a real note and less like a sequence.

```text
Subject: [topic]

[First name] - saw you were engaging with [topic].

We keep hearing the same issue from [their company type] teams, especially when [specific nuance].

Happy to send over the short version of what seems to be working if useful.
```

### Make it stronger

- mention the topic, not every micro-signal
- use plain language
- keep the first email focused on one point
- sound like a person, not a sequence

## LinkedIn best practices

Use LinkedIn when you want a softer first touch around a topic the lead already engaged with.

### Keep connection requests light

If you are not connected yet, do not use the connection note as a full pitch.

Better connection notes:

- reference the topic briefly
- give a real reason to connect
- avoid a hard ask

Example:

```text
Hi [First name], your take on [topic] caught my eye. We work with teams on [related outcome] and I thought it made sense to connect.
```

### Use direct messages for follow-up, not pressure

After you connect, send a short note that adds context or value.

Example:

```text
Thanks for connecting. You were engaging with [topic], and we see a lot of teams hit [problem] there. Happy to share the short playbook we use if that would be useful.
```

### Stay within platform norms

Thoughtful invitations and relevant messages work better than high volume. LinkedIn also expects you to avoid spammy behavior on the platform.

## When not to reach out

Hold back when:

- the person engaged with a sensitive or emotional topic where a pitch would feel tone-deaf
- the signal came from someone who is clearly outside the buying path and there is no reasonable route to a decision-maker
- your team already contacted this person recently through another campaign or channel
- the engagement was clearly drive-by noise, such as a single reaction on a high-volume post with no stronger follow-through

Sometimes the right move is to keep monitoring instead of forcing a first touch.

## A practical starting sequence

Use this as a starting point, not a rule:

1. Day 0: email tied to the signal
2. Day 2 or 3: LinkedIn connection request or short DM
3. Day 5: email follow-up with a new angle, example, or resource
4. Day 8 or 9: LinkedIn follow-up with one simple question
5. Day 12: close the loop with a short final message

Keep each touch different. Every message should earn its place.

## Benchmarks and what good looks like

Use benchmarks to compare signal types and process quality, not to chase vanity numbers.

- Use your first 30 days to set an internal baseline by signal type and freshness tier.
- Signal-based outreach should beat your cold outbound baseline on both reply quality and meeting quality.
- Hot signals should outperform warm and cooling signals.
- Comments, shares, repeat engagement, and multi-person company engagement should outperform one-off reactions.
- Your team should work hot signals within the same day or next business day, and all active signals within 48 hours.
- Signals older than two weeks should usually perform more like cold outbound than like fresh signal-based outreach.

If those patterns are not showing up, your signal selection or messaging is off.

## Measure signal quality over time

Close the loop on every signal you work.

- Tag outreach by signal type, such as comment, repeat engagement, share, or company cluster.
- Track reply rate by signal type and freshness tier.
- Track meeting rate by signal type and freshness tier.
- Review which signals create replies but not meetings. Those signals may show curiosity without real buying fit.
- Drop signals that repeatedly underperform. Not every engagement pattern deserves your team’s time.

## Team coordination

If multiple people work the same accounts, coordinate before you send.

- Assign ownership by account or by signal type.
- Do not let two people from your team reach out to the same person about the same signal.
- When several people from one target company engage, coordinate the motion by seniority and role.
- Use a shared queue, CRM owner, or clear status tags so everyone knows which lead is already being worked.

## Ideas to test

Try these variations:

- segment by signal type, such as comments, repeat engagement, or multi-person company engagement
- change the first call to action from meeting-based to interest-based
- send email first for strong ICP matches and LinkedIn first for lighter signals
- build different angles for thought leadership posts versus product or problem posts
- prioritize repeat engagers for faster follow-up

## Mistakes to avoid

Avoid these common problems:

- leading with a generic pitch instead of the signal
- mentioning small actions in a way that feels invasive
- asking for too much in the first touch
- copying the same message into email and LinkedIn
- following up without adding anything new
- working weak-fit leads just because they engaged once

## Suggested outbound principles for traxy users

Use `traxy` to help you stay selective.

- Start with strong ICP matches, not every engaged person.
- Match the outreach angle to the post or topic that created the signal.
- Move faster on repeat engagement and company clusters.
- Use manual review when the signal is interesting but not yet clear enough for automatic action.
